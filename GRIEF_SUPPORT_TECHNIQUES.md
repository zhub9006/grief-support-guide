# Evidence-Based Grief Support Techniques

> A concise reference for volunteer coordinators summarizing the most research-backed approaches to grief support.

---

## Summary

This guide covers seven evidence-based grief support techniques: **Complicated Grief Therapy (CGT)**, **Cognitive Behavioral Therapy for Grief (CBT-G)**, **Narrative Therapy**, **Grief Support Groups**, **Grief Counseling**, **Eye Movement Desensitization and Reprocessing (EMDR)**, and **Acceptance and Commitment Therapy (ACT)**. Each technique is rated by its current evidence level — Strong, Moderate, or Emerging — based on systematic reviews, meta-analyses, and randomized controlled trials. The strongest evidence supports CGT and CBT-G; narrative therapy and support groups show moderate but growing support; EMDR and ACT are promising but still emerging. Volunteers should match techniques to each bereaved person's needs, culture, and severity of grief.

---

## Techniques

### 1. Complicated Grief Therapy (CGT)

| Field | Detail |
|---|---|
| **Evidence Level** | 🟢 Strong |
| **Key Research** | Shear et al. (2011, *JAMA Psychiatry*); multiple RCTs; recognized as first-line treatment for Prolonged Grief Disorder (PGD) |
| **Description** | A 16-session, manualized treatment specifically designed for Prolonged Grief Disorder. CGT combines elements of attachment theory, CBT, and interpersonal therapy. It uses "revisiting" exercises (re-telling the story of the death), situational revisiting (graded exposure to avoided situations), and future-oriented goal setting. RCTs consistently show CGT outperforms standard grief counseling and CBT for PGD, with large effect sizes. |

### 2. Cognitive Behavioral Therapy for Grief (CBT-G)

| Field | Detail |
|---|---|
| **Evidence Level** | 🟢 Strong |
| **Key Research** | Boelen & van den Hout (2013); systematic reviews and meta-analyses (2023–2025) confirm efficacy for grief symptoms |
| **Description** | CBT adapted for bereavement focuses on identifying and restructuring maladaptive thoughts about the loss (e.g., guilt, hopelessness), reducing avoidance behaviors, and promoting gradual re-engagement with meaningful activities. Exposure-based components help process painful memories. Meta-analyses show moderate-to-large effect sizes for reducing grief severity, depression, and anxiety. |

### 3. Narrative Therapy

| Field | Detail |
|---|---|
| **Evidence Level** | 🟡 Moderate |
| **Key Research** | Santos & Soares (2024) systematic review of 21 studies; art-and-narrative group therapy (Sage, 2022); constructivist-narrative strategies |
| **Description** | Narrative therapy helps bereaved individuals "re-author" their grief story, integrating the loss into a coherent identity rather than being defined by it. Techniques include externalizing grief, constructing alternative narratives, and exploring the ongoing bond with the deceased. A 2024 systematic review found constructivist-narrative strategies effective across diverse loss types, though more RCTs are needed. Particularly well-suited for group formats and culturally diverse populations. |

### 4. Grief Support Groups

| Field | Detail |
|---|---|
| **Evidence Level** | 🟡 Moderate |
| **Key Research** | Meta-analysis of bereavement groups (Tandfonline, 2020); group therapy for PGD review (2025); iResearchNet evidence base |
| **Description** | Structured or semi-structured group settings where bereaved individuals share experiences, normalize emotional responses, and build coping skills together. Mechanisms of change include universality (reducing isolation), emotional expression, and peer modeling. Meta-analyses show small-to-moderate effect sizes for reducing grief and depression symptoms, with stronger effects for groups that are time-limited, facilitated by trained professionals, and targeted at specific loss types (e.g., spousal bereavement, child loss). |

### 5. Grief Counseling

| Field | Detail |
|---|---|
| **Evidence Level** | 🟡 Moderate |
| **Key Research** | Broad clinical literature; Currier et al. (2008) meta-analysis; distinction between uncomplicated and complicated grief |
| **Description** | Individual counseling focused on processing loss, exploring emotions, and adapting to life after bereavement. Typically short-term (6–12 sessions). Most effective for individuals with uncomplicated grief who seek support within the first few months of loss. Evidence suggests grief counseling is less effective than CGT for Prolonged Grief Disorder, but it remains a valuable first-line option for normative grief and as a stepping stone to more intensive treatment. |

### 6. Eye Movement Desensitization and Reprocessing (EMDR)

| Field | Detail |
|---|---|
| **Evidence Level** | 🟠 Emerging |
| **Key Research** | Small RCTs and case-series studies; included in ICANotes (2024) clinical review for traumatic grief |
| **Description** | Originally developed for PTSD, EMDR is increasingly applied to traumatic or sudden bereavement where the death was violent, unexpected, or witnessed. The protocol uses bilateral stimulation (eye movements, tapping) while the client recalls distressing memories of the death. Early studies show reductions in intrusive imagery and grief intensity, but larger RCTs are needed. Best used as an adjunct to grief-specific therapies. |

### 7. Acceptance and Commitment Therapy (ACT)

| Field | Detail |
|---|---|
| **Evidence Level** | 🟠 Emerging |
| **Key Research** | ICANotes (2024) clinical review; small pilot studies for bereavement populations |
| **Description** | ACT focuses on psychological flexibility — helping bereaved individuals accept painful emotions without avoidance, while committing to values-driven actions. Techniques include mindfulness exercises, cognitive defusion, and values clarification. Early evidence suggests ACT may reduce experiential avoidance and improve adjustment after loss, particularly for individuals who struggle with prolonged avoidance of grief emotions. More controlled trials are needed. |

## Evidence-Level Key

| Symbol | Level | Meaning |
|---|---|---|
| 🟢 | **Strong** | Supported by multiple RCTs and/or meta-analyses with consistent, significant effects |
| 🟡 | **Moderate** | Supported by systematic reviews, some RCTs, or large observational studies; effects are real but more research is warranted |
| 🟠 | **Emerging** | Supported by pilot studies, case series, or theoretical rationale; promising but not yet confirmed by large-scale trials |
